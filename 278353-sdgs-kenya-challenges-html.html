SDGs in Kenya and their challenges

July 2024 · 20 minute read

Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) or Global Goals are a universal function to end poverty, preserve the planet, and foster peace and prosperity among nations. SDGs were created at the United Nations Conference on Sustainable Development that was held in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil, in 2012. The main reason behind their creation was to generate a concise set of global goals that would tackle the current economic, political, and environmental challenges facing all nations worldwide. SDGs in Kenya were adopted in January 2016 and have made remarkable changes in the country in under two years.

The 17 Sustainable development goals build on the Millennium development goals (MDGs) and focus on vital areas such as climate change, economic inequality, peace and justice, sustainable consumption, innovation, among other global priorities. SDGs are a furtherance of what was started in the MDGs and a bold commitment to finish what was started while adjusting to incorporate pressing challenges in the world today.

Sustainable development goals list

1. To eradicate poverty in all its forms in the entire country

The current definition of a poor person is anyone living on less than $1.25 (approx. 125 Ksh) daily, handicapping their ability to access sufficient food and clean water and sanitation. Poverty eradication remains to be a major challenge in Kenya and globally. Global efforts saw the number of people living in extreme poverty reduce from 1.9 billion to 836 million between 1990 and 2015.

2. To combat hunger through sustainable agriculture, improved food security, and better nutrition

Kenya’s economic backbone is agriculture. The implementation of SDGs in Kenya pertaining the eradication of hunger involves programs such helping farmers construct greenhouses, partly funding fish farming projects, distribution of free fertilizers for tea and coffee farmers, giving free seeds to arable farmers and so on. The government is also involved in making water available to farmers through the construction of new pipelines, building dams, and so on. The development programs are geared towards combating hunger and improving the countries economy.

3. Reduce the mortality rate through ensuring healthy citizens across all ages

Over 6 million children die before their 5th birthday globally. In Kenya, the number of children who die from preventable diseases such as Malaria and Tuberculosis has reduced drastically between 1990 and 2015. To combat child deaths from preventable diseases, the government in conjunction with the private sector has established programs such as immunization, treatment, and education on sexual and reproductive healthcare.

4. Improve on education by ensuring inclusive and equitable learning that offers lifelong opportunities for all

The achievement of this objective requires the combined contribution of other objectives on the health sector, gender and equality, and so on. Once child mortality rate increases, there will be more girls in school. Similarly, gender equality has to be campaigned for so that girls are included in the learning programs.

5. Achieve gender equality to empower women and girls

Statistics have it that empowering women and girls has a multiplier effect when it comes to development. Eradicating all forms of discrimination against women and girls is no just giving them their human right, it is a fundamental accelerator of the growth and development for Kenya. The fruits of fighting for gender equality in Kenya are vivid in primary schools where gender parity has been achieved.

6. Ensure efficient water and sanitation by improving on its availability and sustainability

The rise of global temperatures as a result of the climatic change is projected to increase water scarcity to over 40 percent of the world’s population. The good news is that 2.1 billion people have access to clean water and sanitation between 1990 and 2015. In Kenya, clean water and sanitation is not a major problem as a majority of the country’s population can access it.

7. Ensure access to reliable, sustainable, and affordable modern energy for all

Kenya’s efforts to ensure clean and affordable energy will involve not just hydroelectricity, but solar, wind, and thermal. Adopting newer technologies will help reduce the cost of electricity consumed in the country significantly. The global figure of people who had access to electricity increased by 1.7 billion between 1990 and 2010.

8. Promote sustainable economic growth through sustained, inclusive, full, productive, and decent employment for all

Economic crises have over the years challenged global efforts to provide sustained economic growth. An example is the 2008 global recession which many nations including Kenya are still recovering from. The post-election violence of 2008 is another economic setback the country encountered and is yet to recover from fully.

9. Foster innovation by promoting inclusive and sustainable industrialization through the establishment of resilient infrastructure

Infrastructure and innovation drive economic growth and sustainable development. Globally, over half the world population now lives in cities, and there are mass transportation systems, and the use of renewable energy sources has become a matter of importance. New industries are created, and information and communication technologies are on the rise.

10. Reduce inequality rates within the country and outside its borders

Statistics have it that the richest 10 percent earn up to 40 percent of global income while the poorest 10 percent earn between 2 percent and 7 percent. The statistics show that inequality is on the rise. In Kenya, inequality has increased by 11 percent, accounting for population growth. The only way to counter the rise in inequality levels is to adopt sound policies that will ensure a balance in age, gender, and race.

11. Create safe, inclusive, sustainable, and resilient human settlements by building cities

The number of people living in cities globally is projected to rise to 6.5 billion (two-thirds of all humanity) by 2050. The Kenyan government has city building projects that will see the number of people living in cities increase significantly. Urbanization is usually a result of economic growth, which implies that the country is on the path of achieving its SDGs on economic development.

12. Ensure stable and sustainable production and consumption patterns

The achievement of economic growth and sustainable development heavily relies on consistent production and consumption patterns. One way of having better patters is by recycling and reusing waste products to create useful items. This both reduces pollutants and enhances a sound production-consumption pattern. In Kenya, agriculture is the biggest consumer of water, claiming over 70 percent of fresh water usage.

13. Establish combative approaches to the challenge of climatic change and its impacts

Despite global efforts to combat the greenhouse effect, the level of greenhouse gas emissions is currently 50 percent higher than in 1990. Global warming is resulting in near-permanent climatic changes that require immediate action. Kenya is not marked as one of the hot ones that require immediate attention, but the country has its fair share of greenhouse gas emissions from industries and petroleum engines.

14. Foster development through the conservation and sustainable use of oceans, seas, and marine resources

Human life requires both the land and oceans for growth and sustenance. The management of these vital resources is a vital concern for humanity as a whole. It is also important to counterbalance the effects of climatic change primarily by preventing pollution in natural resources. In Kenya, the cleansing of Nairobi River water was a major move to ensuring the conservation of the countries natural resources.

15. Promote, protect, and restore the use of sustainable utilization of terrestrial ecosystems by the management of forests, combating diversification, halting, and reversing land degradation, and ending biodiversity loss

Kenya heavily relies on agriculture, not only for food but also for the economy. The need to protect ecosystems and reduce biodiversity loss is as much a local concern as it is a global one. This SDG aims at conserving and restoring the use of terrestrial ecosystems (wetlands, drylands, mountains, and forests).

16. Promote sustainable development through fostering peaceful coexistence, promoting justice to all, and establishing inclusive, accountable, and effective institutions at all levels

Sustainable development is built on the foundation of peace, effective governance, human rights, and stability. Conflict and violence have in the past proved to be detrimental to sustainable development. All forms of sexual violence, torture, crime, and exploitation have a lasting effect on those who are involved, affecting their contribution to the country’s productivity.

17. Enhance the methods of implementation and revive global partnerships for sustainable development

The achievement of SDGs is only through a strong bond between countries and stakeholders. Statistics have it that the official development assistance from developed countries to developing countries increased by 66 percent between 2000 and 2014. However, internal conflicts such as Kenya’s 2008 post-election violence demand more financial aid in repairing the damage.

In the next 15 years, Kenya aims to focus on achieving the 17 critical sustainable development goals. The goals are categories in 5 main areas. They are yet to be adopted by the country under the current leadership of President Uhuru Kenyatta. The total targets are 169 but are narrowed down to 17 key goals.

Financial constraints

The biggest challenge to the implementation of SDGs in Kenya the SDG’s is financing, as reported by Gideon Mailu. He adds that developed countries committed 0.75 percent of their budget to help developing countries, a matter that has not materialized. Mailu denoted that successful development programs are funded mainly by donors. Take an example of the country’s healthcare programs. Over 50 percent of Kenya’s healthcare programs are donor-funded which has contributed to the growth and development of the sector.

Insufficient sustainability of available solutions

While most of the SDGSs are achievable, we still face the trouble of delivering sustainable solutions that will ensure the problems the development goals are addressing won’t come to haunt us in future. Sustainability is the main concept in sustainable development goals. The fact that our country is still chasing after curing symptoms and not addressing causes makes it hard to fully implement these goals. For instance, supplying balanced diet food to communities that face perennial hunger will not address the solution in a sustainable way yet we are still caught in such races.

Medium Term Plans (MTP)

Term Plans were created as a short-term means of meeting long-term objectives. MTPs are a series of successive 5-year plans that will steer the country to meeting Vision 2030.

MTP (2008-2012)

The goal of the first Medium Term Plan of 2008-2012 was to implement flagship projects that were identified in accordance to Vision 2030. Other major programs and policies over the previous 5 years were also included. On financing and investment of the programs, the greater share of finances came from the private sector under the Public Private Partnerships (PPP) program. The ratio of domestic savings to GDP was projected to increase from 16 percent to 26 percent while that of investment to GDP was projected to increase by 10 percent in 5 years.

Public-Private Partnership (PPP/P3/P3)

Public-Private Partnership a joint venture between the government and the private sector, whereby both contribute to achieving a goal of common interest. The private sector, in this case, may be one or more private businesses such as private companies. A PPP program implies a contract between a public company and a private corporation whereby the private party agrees to oversee a project, assuming full responsibility for the financial, technical, and operational risks. The president assented the Public-Private Partnerships Bill of 2012 in January 2013. The bill sought to facilitate key development projects by increasing the funding capacity. At the moment, the country has up to 6 major projects entailing infrastructure, energy, transport and others, all with a combined total of 4 Trillion Kenya Shillings.

Sustainable Development Goals in Kenya and Vision 2030

The Vision 2030 is Kenya’s development blueprint that started in 2008 and ends in 2030. Kenya’s Vision 2030 runs concurrently with UN’s Sustainable Development Goals that began in January 2012 and last for 15 years (end in 2027). Kenya’s Vision 2030 is similar in an endeavor to SDGs in that its aims to transform the country into a middle-income country that provides quality life to all its citizens by 2030 through avid industrialization.

Kenya’s Vision 2030 is subdivided into three pillars:

1. The Political Pillar

The pillar aims to establish a fully democratic system that respects the rule of law and administers justice to every Kenyan citizen regardless of social status.

2. The Social Pillar

The social pillar endeavors to establish a just and cohesive society that lives in a clean environment.

3. The Economic Pillar

The economic pillar aims at fostering prosperity for all Kenyan citizens via an economic development program that spans across all regions of the country. An example of how this pillar is to be realized is the plan to build a futuristic metropolitan city named Konza. The economic pillar aims at achieving a GDP growth rate of 10 percent per year starting 2012.

SDGs launch in Kenya

Gideon Mailu claims that Kenya began its Millennium Development Goals (MDG) four years late, back in 2004, and thus the Ministry of Devolution is trying to avoid the same mistake by starting on Early October 2015. The MDGs were 8 in total with 21 targets that governments all over the world promised to achieve between 2000 and 2015. MDGs were then succeeded by the currently undergoing SDGs when MDGs reached their expiration period. The director of the devolution insisted that Kenya must out-perform other countries in the world in the realization of these SDGs as it led the world in creating them. This worldwide process of creating new goals was co-chaired by Dr. Macharia Kamau, the permanent representative to the United Nations. Head of Kenya Manufacturers Association, Betty Maina, sat on the panel of eminent persons during the post-2015 development agenda for the East and South African region, as appointed by the secretary to the United Nations in 2012. A factor attributed to the success of the first MDGs was the active participation of donors in contributing money to end issues like maternal and child deaths.
